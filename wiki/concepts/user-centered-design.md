# user-centered design

User-centered design (UCD) is a design methodology that prioritizes the needs, preferences, and limitations of end users throughout the development process. The approach involves actively involving users at every stage—from research and ideation to prototyping and testing—to ensure the final product is highly usable and relevant to its target audience.

A key principle of UCD is iterative design, where feedback from users is continuously gathered and integrated to refine the product. This contrasts with traditional design approaches that often focus on technical feasibility or aesthetic considerations without direct user input. By centering the design process on users, UCD aims to improve user satisfaction, accessibility, and overall experience.

In practice, UCD employs various techniques such as user interviews, surveys, usability testing, and co-creation workshops. For example, Adobe’s development of Acrobat Spaces—a free AI-powered study tool for students—illustrates UCD principles by incorporating feedback from students at leading universities like Harvard, Berkeley, and Brown. This involvement helped Adobe tailor features such as AI chat assistance and podcast generation to better meet student needs, demonstrating how user insights can guide product innovation.

User-centered design is widely applied in software development, web design, and product engineering, often overlapping with fields like [[human-computer interaction]] and [[experience design]]. Its focus on empathy and usability makes it a foundational approach for creating effective, user-friendly technologies.
